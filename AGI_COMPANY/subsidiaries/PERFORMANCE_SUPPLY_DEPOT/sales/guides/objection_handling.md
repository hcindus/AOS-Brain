## The "Feel, Felt, Found" Framework

The "Feel, Felt, Found" technique is a powerful way to handle objections with empathy while guiding prospects toward a solution. It validates their concern without being argumentative.

### The Structure:
1. **FEEL** - Acknowledge and validate their feeling
2. **FELT** - Show they're not alone (others felt this way too)
3. **FOUND** - Share what others discovered after moving forward

### Example:
> Prospect: "I think you're too expensive."
> 
> Response: "I completely understand why you'd **feel** that way — price is a real consideration. A lot of our clients actually **felt** the same way when they first looked at our pricing. What they **found** was that the time savings and efficiency gains paid for the investment within [timeframe], and they ended up saving [amount] in the first year alone."

## Common Objections & Responses

### 1. "Your Price Is Too High"

**Feel:**
"I totally understand — budget is always a factor, and I appreciate you being upfront about it."

**Felt:**
"Most of our clients actually felt the same way when they first saw our pricing. They had existing budgets and weren't sure they could justify the investment."

**Found:**
"What they found was that [specific ROI outcome] — for example, one client in [industry] saw a [X]% reduction in [metric] within [timeframe], which translated to $[amount] in savings. The system essentially paid for itself in [timeframe]. Would it help if I walked you through how we calculate ROI for companies like yours?"

**Alternative Responses:**
- "Can I ask what you're comparing us to? Sometimes it's not apples to apples."
- "What budget are you working with? We might be able to phase the implementation."
- "What would you need to see to feel the price is justified?"

---

### 2. "We Don't Have Budget Right Now"

**Feel:**
"I completely understand — the timing of budget cycles can be really tricky."

**Felt:**
"We've had other clients who felt the same way, especially when they discovered us mid-year or outside their planning cycle."

**Found:**
"What they found was that sometimes waiting for the next budget cycle meant [cost of waiting: lost efficiency, missed opportunities, compounding problems]. We've also been able to structure pilot programs that fit within existing budgets. Would it make sense to explore what a phased approach might look like?"

**Alternative Responses:**
- "When does your fiscal year start? I can follow up then."
- "Is this a priority for next year's budget?"
- "Would a pilot program with a smaller investment make sense to start?"

---

### 3. "We're Already Using [Competitor]"

**Feel:**
"That makes sense — if you've already got something in place, it shows you're serious about this area."

**Felt:**
"A lot of our current clients were actually using [competitor] before they came to us. They felt like the solution was okay but weren't sure if they were getting everything they needed."

**Found:**
"What they found was that [specific differentiator: our AI accuracy, our support, our integration capabilities, our pricing model] made a meaningful difference in their results. One client switched from [competitor] and saw [specific improvement]. Would you be open to a comparison — just to make sure you're not missing out on something better?"

**Alternative Responses:**
- "Are you happy with how it's going?"
- "What's working well with your current solution?"
- "What's one thing you wish your current solution did better?"

---

### 4. "I Need to Think About It"

**Feel:**
"Absolutely — this is a decision that deserves careful thought."

**Felt:**
"Most people feel that way before making a commitment like this. They want to make sure they're doing the right thing for their business."

**Found:**
"What I've found helps people get clarity is identifying what's still unclear. Is there a specific question or concern that's holding you back? I'd rather address it now than have you stewing on it."

**Alternative Responses:**
- "What specifically do you need to think through?"
- "Is there information I can provide to help with that?"
- "Who else needs to be involved in this decision?"

---

### 5. "I Need to Run This By [Decision Maker/Boss/Team]"

**Feel:**
"Of course — that's totally reasonable, especially for a decision like this."

**Felt:**
"Most of our clients need to involve others in the decision. They felt a bit uncertain about how to present it to their team or leadership."

**Found:**
"What they found helpful was having me join a brief call with the decision maker to answer questions directly. That often speeds things up and makes sure everyone has the same information. Would that work, or would you prefer I send some materials you can share?"

**Alternative Responses:**
- "Who else is involved in this decision?"
- "What questions do you think they'll have?"
- "What would convince them this is the right move?"

---

### 6. "The Timing Isn't Right"

**Feel:**
"I hear you — timing is everything, and I don't want to push you into something when you're not ready."

**Felt:**
"A lot of prospects felt that way when we first connected. They were dealing with [busy season / other priorities / recent changes]."

**Found:**
"What some of them found, though, was that waiting actually cost them more in the long run — either in missed opportunities or in the problem getting bigger. Others found that even starting with a small pilot made sense. When do you think the timing would be better?"

**Alternative Responses:**
- "What's happening that's making the timing off?"
- "When would be a better time to revisit this?"
- "Is there a smaller step we could take now?"

---

### 7. "We're Too Small for AI"

**Feel:**
"I appreciate that perspective — it's a common concern."

**Felt:**
"A lot of smaller companies felt that way initially. They thought AI was only for the big enterprises with massive budgets and IT departments."

**Found:**
"What they found was that our solution is actually designed to scale with businesses — we have plenty of clients with [X employees/revenue size] who are getting huge value. In fact, smaller companies often see the biggest impact because it helps them punch above their weight. One client with just [size] saved [amount] hours per week on [task]. Can I show you what that might look like for you?"

**Alternative Responses:**
- "What's your biggest time sink right now?"
- "How much time does your team spend on [manual task]?"
- "What if you could [specific benefit] without adding headcount?"

---

### 8. "Your Solution Is Too Complex"

**Feel:**
"I completely understand that concern — nobody wants to take on a complicated implementation."

**Felt:**
"Many of our clients felt that way at first, especially if they'd had bad experiences with other tech implementations."

**Found:**
"What they found was that our onboarding process is designed to be straightforward — we handle [setup/training/integration], and most clients are fully up and running within [timeframe]. Plus, our support team is with you every step of the way. Would it help if I walked you through exactly what implementation looks like?"

**Alternative Responses:**
- "What part feels complex to you?"
- "How much time do you have to dedicate to implementation?"
- "We can phase this — would a simpler first step work?"

---

### 9. "I Don't Trust AI / AI Won't Work for My Business"

**Feel:**
"I totally get that — there's a lot of hype around AI, and it's smart to be skeptical."

**Felt:**
"We've had plenty of prospects who felt exactly the same way. They'd seen AI that overpromised and underdelivered, or they weren't sure it would understand their specific business."

**Found:**
"What they found after trying our solution was that [specific proof point: our accuracy rate, our customization, our human-in-the-loop approach] made the difference. One skeptical client in [industry] said after implementation: '[quote/testimonial].' Would you be open to a pilot where you can see it working with your actual data?"

**Alternative Responses:**
- "What concerns you most about AI?"
- "Have you tried AI solutions before?"
- "Would a proof-of-concept with your data help?"

---

### 10. "We're Going to Build This In-House"

**Feel:**
"That makes sense — building in-house can feel like you have more control."

**Felt:**
"We've had companies that felt the same way and started down that path."

**Found:**
"What they found was that building and maintaining an AI solution took [X months] and [X resources] they didn't anticipate — and in the end, the result wasn't as robust as what we offer off-the-shelf. One CTO told me they wished they'd gone with us from the start because it would have saved them [time/money]. Can I share what the build-versus-buy comparison typically looks like?"

**Alternative Responses:**
- "What's your timeline for building this?"
- "Do you have AI/ML expertise in-house already?"
- "What's the opportunity cost of waiting for a build?"

## General Principles

### Do's
- ✅ Listen fully before responding
- ✅ Acknowledge the objection with empathy
- ✅ Ask clarifying questions
- ✅ Use "Feel, Felt, Found" to validate and pivot
- ✅ Focus on value and outcomes, not features
- ✅ Be prepared with specific examples and case studies

### Don'ts
- ❌ Argue or become defensive
- ❌ Talk over the prospect
- ❌ Make promises you can't keep
- ❌ Rush to answer — pause and think
- ❌ Use generic responses without context
- ❌ Pressure or guilt-trip
